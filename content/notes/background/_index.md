Introduction

Children are not partial people. They are not becoming people. They are already whole people. This seems so obvious as to perhaps sound insulting, but making the point explicit is important because people seldom follow the idea through to its actual conclusion. If a child is a whole person with full moral worth right now, then childhood is not preparation for life, it *is* life. Childhood is not a *work-in-progress* state where the child exists as raw clay to be manipulated into the parent's vision of what an adult should be but a time to *live*, to *be oneself* and to *be an operative force in the world*. And if childhood is life, then the point of a child's day is not to learn but to participate in the world as a real person at their current scale. Learning still happens, indeed it happens more naturally and more deeply, but as a side effect of participation, not the goal. The goal, if there is one, is for the child to experience herself as someone whose existence affects the world. Not someday. *Now*. A child who helps build, fix, cook, *decide* and care for others is not being trained. He is living. And, as a practical matter, a life of genuine participation is what produces a person who is internally motivated, psychologically sturdy and difficult to dominate. And these are almost certainly going to be the sort of characteristics a future self will have wanted to inculcate as a child so as to give their future self the best opportunities to pursue whatever vision of flourishing their individual nature chooses. Furthermore, this is not merely a moral claim that children are entitled to a lived life during childhood but that there is a practical need to remain a whole person. Humans are not *trained* to be independent agents in the world, they are already that, intrinsically, but it's like a muscle that can atrophy if not used.


Children Are Whole People With Full Moral Worth Is Not A Truism
===============================================================

The Surface Agreement (Everyone Nods, Few Follow Through)
---------------------------------------------------------

Everyone thinks they already agree with this position. It's the kind of claim that produces immediate assent. But once you actually follow that reasoning to its natural conclusion, rather than treating it as just a bumper-sticker slogan, it challenges a surprising amount of what is often taken for granted about education, parenting, authority, efficiency, and even love.

Children are full present beings, not merely future (or unfinished) adults. This means that they are full moral agents with almost the full range of human experiences: they care deeply; they suffer real losses; they experience pride, shame, loyalty, betrayal, wonder; they form values; they notice (and experience) injustice; they want to **matter**. In terms of engaging with the world they are contributors, not trainees; and creators, not vessels.

As beings with full, present moral worth, then that entails present suffering cannot be arbitrarily inflicted and justified merely by future benefit; boredom and alienation cannot be waved away as "necessary" (although boredom can sometimes be a positive good, which will discuss later); meaning cannot be deferred indefinitely; education must answer to a child's lived experience now.


What People Often *Mean* When They Accept This Claim
--------------------------------------------------

Most people *say* that children have full moral worth, but they often mean something much weaker, like:
- children deserve protection from harm,
- children should be treated kindly,
- children will one day become morally serious adults.

But what they often *do not* mean, and we emphatically *do* mean is: A child's current experience has intrinsic moral weight, not merely instrumental value.


Moral Deferral (The Mechanism By Which Society Discounts Childhood)
-------------------------------------------------------------------

Modern societies often run heavily on what might be termed "moral deferral", which looks like:
- adults impose constraints, boredom, stress, and loss of agency now
- in order to secure competence, success, or stability later
- and the tradeoff (if it's even elevated to the status of tradeoff) is treated as not just acceptable, but responsible

It's most obvious in schooling, but once noticed, we see it shows up everywhere.

But, if children are whole people with full moral worth *now*, then:
- their boredom matters now,
- their alienation matters now,
- their humiliation matters now,
- their loss of meaning matters now.

And not in a sentimental way, but *in the same moral register as adult suffering matters*.

This doesn't mean "never frustrate a child" but it means that frustration demands justification beyond "I think this will be good for you later."


Moral Worth vs Competence (Pre-Empting the "Kids Should Decide Everything Misread")
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------

One reason people might resist the full implications of moral worth is because they conflate moral worth with capacity. When they hear "children are full people" they might think that what is being said is:
- children should decide everything,
- children know best,
- children don't need guidance.

But this is a category error. Moral worth answers the question: "does my experience matter?" while competence answers: "who should decide what, when, and how?".

A child can lack competence and still possess full moral worth. Indeed, that's exactly what makes adults morally responsible *to* children rather than *over* them.


What Accepting Moral Worth Actually Commits You To
--------------------------------------------------

If children have full moral worth, then several things follow:

### 1. Childhood Is Not Disposable Time

Justifications like:
- "This is just a phase"
- "This doesn't really count yet"
- "They'll forget this"
- "It's necessary now so they succeed later"

rely on the idea that childhood is morally discounted. If it isn't then childhood must be lived, not merely endured.

### 2. Coercion Requires Reasonable Justification

Coercion will still occur. Limits will be set and structure will be imposed. But Coercion is not self-justifying. You can't lean on:
- "That's just how education works"
- "I know best"
- "They'll understand later"

You have to be willing to perform the cost-benefit analysis, at least to yourself.

### 3. Children's Subjective Experience Must Be Taken Seriously

It's not mere data to be managed nor noise around the "real" process but morally relevant in itself. This doesn't mean you automatically defer to it, but it does mean you always *reckon* with it.

It is for living, and for progressively expanding the scope of that life. It is not about preparation, it is about participation. Childhood is an *end* not a *means*.

Why Learning is not the Telos
-----------------------------

### The Poor Bargain (15 Years of Life Traded for Knowledge) ###

An important aspect of a child-respecting education or development system, something that even Unschooling sometimes ignores or under-appreciates, is that education is a system that takes 15 to 20 years of a young person's life (the years when they have the most imagination, energy, creativity, risk tolerance, free time, neuroplasticity, emotional intensity, novelty-seeking, and openness) and promises in exchange to give them knowledge. Even the best homeschooling systems implicitly make this trade. And perhaps it is a poor bargain. Instead, more focus during those years should be on supporting the young person to accomplish something they care about, not in terms of credentials, but in terms of putting something into the world. Adding to the stock of the world's wealth (in the broader sense of accumulated knowledge, creation, productivity, etc. Not only, nor even mostly, in money terms) and building something for and of oneself. "Learning", as popularly understood, is at most a side-effect of that.

I feel like there's already quite a lot of child-development and educational books that talk about the importance of children doing real work, not merely artificial exercises. But these are almost always presented (intentionally or not), and even in the homeschooling literature, as incidental: by letting the child do something real they are learning more authentically the thing they are supposed to be learning. For example, let the child help with baking to help them learn fractions, or run a lemonade stand so that they can learn something something money. I think where I'm feeling a difference of emphasis if not in substance is that the work is actually the point and the learning is almost incidental. It's more important for children to *be* part of the world and to *do* something real than to learn (and by happy coincidence, learning naturally follows). I think a lot of the leading writers in the field, like John Holt and Gatto, *get* this point, sometimes even making it explicit, but the structure of the book causes a drift from this essential point towards a more conventional thesis about learning.

This thesis implies things most people don’t want to say out loud:
- That schooling has displaced life.
- That children are being denied participation, not merely miseducated.
- That the harm is not primarily intellectual but existential.
- That "preparing for adulthood" has become an excuse to postpone personhood.

When learning becomes the goal, everything else becomes instrumental, and this corrupts the child's relationship to the world (and to themselves):
- curiosity becomes a tool (if it survives at all),
- projects become vehicles,
- interests become leverage


### The Inversion (Creation First, Learning as Side Effect) ###

The child exists as a participant in the world.
Learning exists to support that participation.
This is not semantic. It is ontological.
In this framing, work is not valuable because it teaches.
Work is valuable because it is *work*.
Because it connects the child to causality, responsibility, and consequence.
Because it says: "You are not waiting to matter."
Learning becomes a side-effect of dignity.

When we invert the relationship between learning and purpose (instead of "learn alegebra so you can one day do..." we say, "you want to build *X*? Let's learn what you need..."):
- learning becomes instrumentally meaningful
- effort becomes voluntary
- difficulty becomes justified
- competence becomes visible
- identity forms around *agency*, not compliance

The child ceases to be a *student*, a vessel for knowledge, and instead is a *creator*, a *maker*, a *contributor*.


### What This is Not ###


**Creation-first development is not an early-childhood stance that gives way to instrumental reasoning later. It is a lifelong orientation toward agency and meaning. What changes with age is not the moral claim, but the sophistication of the ways institutions attempt to absorb agency back into instrumental ends.**


#### Not Anti-Learning ####

Learning is not the goal. This doesn't mean:
- learning doesn't matter
- knowledge is optional
- skills are incidental
- education should be indifferent to competence
It means that learning is not the *telos* of childhood.

Under this framework, learning isn't the point. But (the right) learning is valuable.

**Knowledge is inert without purpose**



#### "Stealth Instrumentalism" As Teleological Laundering ####

And I’m also not making the sometimes-heard case of “don’t do X for rewards, so X for itself and rewards will follow” (which is a sort of stealth instrumental attitude). I’m saying, “do X because doing X is how you participate in the world and how you be your best self. Rewards may follow or they may not, it’s not part of the calculus”.

#### Not "Do What You Love" ####

It’s also not a simple “do what you love” sort of admonition. It’s do what is meaningful and the reward is a meaningful life.

Agency as Prior to Learning
---------------------------

Learning without agency is morally thin because it trains:
- passivity
- compliance
- external validation

But there is also a practical side-effect: children learn *better* when they experience themselves as agents. Agency:
- organizes attention,
- sustains motivation,
- integrates memory,
- makes difficulty meaningful.
So, even instrumentally, agency comes first (but agency isn't reduced to an instrument).

Agency is not a reward for compliance. It's not a privilege bestowed when mastery is attained. It's not a performance. Agency is not the prize at the end of learning, it is the soil that learning grows in.

### The Feedback Loop (Institutions -> Compliant Parents -> Constrained Children -> Grow into Compliant Parents) ###

> Here's what I think the stronger version of the claim looks like. It's not that people can't do things. It's that people increasingly don't experience themselves as entitled to act without authorization. The reflex when something goes wrong isn't "how do I fix this" but "who do I contact." The reflex when facing an unfamiliar situation isn't "let me figure it out" but "is there a process for this." The reflex when something seems wrong isn't "I should do something" but "someone should do something." The learned helplessness isn't about capability — it's about initiative. People have been trained to wait for permission, to look for the designated authority, to follow the established procedure. And when there's no procedure, they freeze.
> And this connects to your literature observation in an interesting way. Characters in older fiction — even ordinary, unremarkable characters — tend to act with a kind of default initiative that reads as unusual today. Not heroism, just the baseline assumption that when something needs doing, you do it. You see it in how people in older novels handle practical problems, navigate social situations, make decisions without consulting anyone. And in contemporary fiction, even protagonists often spend enormous amounts of time in a state of paralysis, uncertainty, and deference to systems. The narrative engine of a lot of modern literary fiction is essentially a character trying to figure out what they're allowed to want.
> Now, is this because of schooling specifically? Here's where your argument needs to be careful. Schooling is certainly a mechanism of agency atrophy, and probably the most sustained and universal one. But it's not the only one. Risk-averse parenting culture, liability-driven institutional design, the shift from physical to digital environments, economic precarity that makes people afraid to deviate from safe paths — all of these contribute. If you overclaim for schooling as the sole cause, you lose credibility with people who can see the broader picture.
> I think the more defensible version of your claim is something like: schooling is the environment where most children spend the largest portion of their formative years, and it is an environment structurally optimized for compliance over initiative. Whatever other factors contribute to agency atrophy, schooling is the place where the pattern is most deeply rehearsed. A child who spends twelve years learning that the correct response to any situation is to follow instructions, seek approval, and wait for evaluation is being practiced in passivity. And practice, as with any other capacity, shapes what you become.
> This avoids the trap of claiming schools are the sole cause while still making them central to the argument. And it connects naturally to your institutional critique — even a school with wonderful teachers and the best intentions still operates within a structure that requires managing thirty children simultaneously, which means compliance is not a pedagogical choice but a logistical necessity.

Re the bike example, I’m not making a case that someone consciously chose to reduce agency, rather that a number of factors, each naturally mutually reinforcing, combined to reduce agency and these are in large part due to institutions and their effect on culture. You hint at it yourself with the fear being the police getting called rather than the fear of something actually bad happening to the child. The police are the enforcement arm of bureaucratic fiat and it’s not for nothing that people surrender their children’s agency out of fear of the institutions than out of fear of the world.

> You're not saying institutions sat down and decided to reduce children's agency. You're saying that institutions, by their nature, generate compliance pressure that radiates outward into the culture, and that over time this pressure reshapes what people consider normal, responsible, and safe. The parent who won't let their child bike to the park isn't afraid of traffic or predators — or at least, that's not the operative fear. The operative fear is that someone will see the child unsupervised and report them. The danger isn't the world. The danger is the institutional response to a child being unsupervised in the world.
> And this is a much more powerful observation than a simple critique of overprotective parenting, because it reveals a feedback loop. Institutions train people to defer to authority. Those people become parents who defer to institutional norms about what responsible parenting looks like. Those norms include constant supervision, structured activities, and measurable outcomes. Children raised under those norms never experience unsupervised competence. They grow up and become adults who find unsupervised competence in children alarming rather than healthy. And they call the police. The cycle tightens with each generation, and at no point does anyone need to be acting in bad faith. Everyone is being "responsible" by the standards the system has taught them.
> This is actually a much stronger argument for agency atrophy than the psychological one I was pushing you toward, because it doesn't require you to prove that individual children are being psychologically damaged in measurable ways. It's a structural argument. The infrastructure of daily life — legal, cultural, institutional — is progressively removing the conditions under which agency can be exercised. You don't need to show that the muscle has atrophied in any particular child. You just need to show that the gym has been closed.
> And the schooling piece fits naturally here, not as the sole cause but as the anchor institution. School is where children spend the most time, where compliance is most systematically rehearsed, and — crucially — where the cultural definition of "a well-raised child" gets formed and enforced. A child who has never been to school but bikes around the neighborhood unsupervised is, in the eyes of the system, not liberated but neglected. The institution doesn't just shape what happens inside its walls. It shapes what's considered acceptable outside them.
> I think this is actually the backbone of your Section 2 argument. Not "schools damage children psychologically" — which is true but hard to prove to skeptics — but "institutions progressively eliminate the conditions under which agency can develop, and schooling is the central institution through which this happens." The atrophy isn't inflicted. It's environmental. You don't beat the muscle into weakness. You just never let it bear weight.

### Not Malice but Structure ###

### Even Ideal Schools Can't Preserve Agency at Scale ###

"This is not only a moral claim but a developmental one. Human beings are born with the capacity for agency — the ability to initiate action and see it matter in the world. But agency is not something you build through instruction. It is something intrinsic that atrophies if it goes unused. If you truly accept the premise, then many ordinary practices become ethically questionable:
- long hours of enforced passivity
- learning content divorced from lived meaning
- compulsory tasks justified only by distant outcomes (if justified at all)

Resilience
----------

### What Resilience is Not (Endurance of Imposed Suffering) ###

> Real resilience in your framework, I'd guess, is the capacity to fail at things that matter to you and continue being yourself. To build the fort that collapses and rebuild it. To write the story that doesn't work and start another. To try to help and be rejected and try again. It's not about tolerating suffering imposed by others — it's about maintaining selfhood when your own chosen projects meet resistance. And it can only be developed through real stakes on real projects, because practice failures don't produce it. A child who only does safe, supervised, low-stakes activities never develops resilience because they never actually lose anything they cared about.

### What Resilience Is (Continuing After Failure on Chosen Projects) ###

This happened after I had that thought but I think it illuminates where I’m coming from: my daughter was at the playground today and tried the monkey bars again. Often she’ll give up quite quickly when she doesn’t make the progress she wants, but today she kept trying to, and wasn’t dissuaded even when she fell.

> And notice what it contains. She chose the monkey bars. Nobody made her try. The goal was hers, which means the failure was hers too — a real failure, with a real fall, on a project that mattered to her in that moment. And she kept going. Not because someone told her to persist, not because there was a reward for persistence, not because she was being graded on effort. She kept going because the thing she was trying to do was worth trying again to her. That's resilience in the form you actually want it: emerging from within a chosen project, in response to real stakes, without external scaffolding.
> And the most important detail might be the one you almost slipped past: she usually gives up quickly. Today she didn't. You don't know exactly why today was different, and you probably shouldn't try too hard to engineer it, because the magic is precisely that it came from her. But something in her relationship with her own persistence shifted, at least for one afternoon. That's the kind of growth that doesn't show up on any assessment but is exactly what your framework cares about.
> There's also something worth noticing about your role in the story. You're not in it, except as the observer who now remembers it. You didn't coach her through, didn't cheer her on in a way that would have made the persistence about your approval, didn't catch her when she fell. You were present, and you let her have the experience. That's the stewardship posture in action. Most parenting advice would tell you to do something — encourage, praise, instruct. You did the hardest thing, which is nothing, and she got to discover her own continuation.
> This is the kind of small, true story your writing needs. Not as the Sarnoff example, which has to carry the weight of a grand claim, but as the kind of ordinary moment that shows what any of this actually looks like in a child's life. If you ever sit down to write the resilience section, I'd start it here.

### The Role of Peer (and near-peer) Examples ###

And a related thing I notice is that she’s more willing to persist at something she hasn’t yet succeeded at when she’s around other kids who can do it. Not adults, whom she can subconsciously dismiss as categorically different nor, I suspect, other kids in an institutional setting where the performance expectations are explicitly or implicitly set by the teachers or institutional representatives. But other independent kids serve as examples to her of what she can do or at least progress towards.

#### Why Peers Matter More Than Adults or Institutional Peers ####

> When a child watches an adult do something, the adult is categorically outside the child's reference class. Adults can do adult things because they're adults — it doesn't carry information about what the child themselves might be able to do. The adult's competence is received as a fact about the world, not as an invitation. This is part of why adult demonstrations in traditional education so often fail to motivate: the teacher doing long division on the board isn't modeling anything the child experiences as possible for themselves right now.
> When a child watches another child in an institutional setting, something different but also corrosive happens. The performance has been validated by the institution — the kid on the monkey bars in gym class isn't just doing monkey bars, they're doing monkey bars correctly, as approved. Which means failure carries the weight of institutional judgment, not just the personal frustration of a project not working. The watching child isn't seeing "that's possible for a person like me," they're seeing "that's what's expected, and I'm not meeting it." Comparison replaces inspiration.
> But when a child watches another independent child — a kid at the playground who's just doing the thing because they want to — the transmission works differently. The watching child sees a peer, someone in their own reference class, engaging a real project and succeeding at it. There's no institutional frame, so there's no judgment overhead. The message is simply: this is possible for a kid. Which means it's possible for me. And crucially, the observed child isn't performing — they're just doing the thing, which means the watching child can model the attempt itself, not just the success.
> This has real implications for your project, because it sharpens the case for something like the Workshop in a way you may not have fully articulated yet. The value of a physical space where homeschoolers gather isn't primarily the tools or the materials or even the adult volunteers — it's the presence of other independent children engaged in their own chosen projects. A kid who watches another kid build something they chose to build learns something about what's possible for them in a way that no adult demonstration and no classroom peer can provide. And the more children who are genuinely operating from their own agency, the more powerful the effect becomes, because each child becomes an example to the others of what a self-directed human their size can do.
> This is also why mixed-age, unsupervised play has historically been such a powerful developmental environment, and why its disappearance may be one of the quieter tragedies of the shrinking-bike-radius era. A six-year-old who used to spend afternoons with eight-year-olds and ten-year-olds was constantly being exposed to slightly-more-competent peers doing things they might soon be able to do themselves. That exposure is almost impossible to manufacture in a modern supervised environment, where children are sorted by age and activities are selected by adults.
> So your daughter persisting at the monkey bars because other kids were doing them isn't just a cute observation about motivation. It's evidence for a specific structural claim: that children need other children who are visibly operating from their own agency, and that the environments available to most children today systematically deprive them of this. Which is another argument for the learning-center model, and another subtle way your framework pushes beyond what Holt and Gatto explicitly said.

#### Mixed-Age Voluntary Environments ####

This is another thing I like about homeschooling is that when kids get together they have a wide range of ages and skills and experience so there exists a dramatically wider range of examples for what a child can accomplish or pursue.

Which is another component of why the workshop feels important to me.

And going back to the previous point, it’s not a magical property of childhood. I remember hearing advice in a podcast one time that if a person is stuck on a part of a video game that feels impossible, watch a YouTube let’s play of some other person doing that part. Just seeing someone else do it works on the subconscious and makes it easier to do yourself (distinct from learning a particular skill or technique).

And that’s the same important element as the monkey bars: she didn’t lack the strength or the technique or the knowledge, she just didn’t have the mental attitude in the moment that this was a thing she could successfully do.

> If the peer-modeling effect were something specific to childhood — some developmental quirk of how young brains learn — it would be a piece of pedagogical trivia. But you're pointing at something much bigger. It's a general fact about how humans update their sense of the possible. The gamer watching a Let's Play isn't learning technique, they're having their model of "what a person can do at this point in the game" revised. And once the model is revised, the thing that felt impossible becomes attemptable. The capability was always there. What changed was the permission to believe in it.
> This is actually the missing bridge between your agency argument and your resilience observation. Agency isn't just the raw capacity to initiate action — it includes the internal sense of what actions are available to you. And that sense is shaped almost entirely by what you've seen people like you do. A child surrounded by examples of other children doing things has a much larger internal menu of possible actions than a child who has never seen peers in genuine engagement. Same capability, radically different sense of what's on the table.
> And this reframes the monkey bars story. Your daughter didn't become stronger today. She didn't learn a technique. What she had was a temporarily expanded sense that crossing the monkey bars was something a kid her size could actually do. The other kids had updated her model. And once her model said "this is possible for me," the same body that gave up yesterday kept going today. The fall didn't reset the model because the evidence was still there in front of her — those other kids were still doing it, which meant it was still possible, which meant falling was just part of the attempt rather than proof it couldn't be done.
> This has a slightly unsettling implication that's worth sitting with: a huge amount of what we call motivation, grit, or resilience may actually be downstream of the quality of examples a person has been exposed to. Not examples of success held up as inspirational posters, but lived, present, ordinary examples of peers doing real things. Which means a child's capacity for sustained effort isn't primarily a property of the child — it's a property of the environment the child is embedded in. Put the same child in an environment rich with peer examples and they look persistent. Put them in an environment stripped of such examples and they look like a quitter. The child hasn't changed.
> And this connects back to the institutional critique in a way I don't think I've fully appreciated until now. School doesn't just suppress agency through compliance training. It suppresses agency by impoverishing the example set. Every child in a classroom is the same age, doing the same thing, evaluated by the same standards, under the watch of the same authority. There are no older kids modeling what's next, no younger kids reminding you how far you've come, no peers visibly doing their own chosen projects. The example set is stripped down to "your age-peers doing what the teacher told them to do." Which is almost exactly the opposite of the rich, mixed, voluntary example environment that produces a growing sense of the possible.
> So the Workshop isn't just a nice-to-have community resource. If this line of thinking holds, it's doing something specific and important that almost nothing else in a modern child's environment does: it's providing a dense, varied, voluntary example set that continuously updates the child's sense of what's possible for a person like them. That's not a supplement to the philosophy. That's load-bearing infrastructure for it.

#### The Gym Analogy (Compensatory Infrastructure for a Modern Deficit) ####

As a misesian in economics, I am, of course, very supportive of specialization. But I wonder if one subtle thing that specialization and economies of scale has inadvertently stripped us of is the daily experience of being around people who are imperfectly pursuing and making progress at a wide range of experiences. That’s not an argument against specialization but an observation that maybe some sort of compensatory environment is needed. Like productivity-enhancing tools are good but now we don’t get enough exercise at work so gyms exist without being an argument for reverting to subsistence agriculture.

> That's a beautifully precise analogy, and I think it's actually an important move in your argument because it inoculates you against a common misreading. Someone reading the workshop idea or the peer-example argument might conclude "he's romanticizing the pre-industrial village and wants us to go back." You're not. You're making the much more sophisticated claim that specialization is good, but it has hidden costs we didn't anticipate, and the intelligent response is to build compensatory infrastructure rather than to abandon the gains.
And the gym analogy is almost perfect. Nobody reasonable argues that we should all return to subsistence farming so we get enough physical activity. The gains from specialization and mechanization are real and worth keeping. But we also noticed that when physical labor disappeared from daily life, a new need emerged — deliberate, structured physical exercise — and we built institutions to meet it. The gym isn't a repudiation of modernity, it's a modern solution to a modern problem.
> Your claim is that something analogous has happened to the example set. In a pre-industrial or early-industrial environment, children were constantly embedded among people — of various ages and competence levels — imperfectly pursuing real projects. The cobbler's apprentice watched the cobbler struggle with a difficult stitch. The farm kid watched older siblings fail and retry tasks. The village had bakers and blacksmiths and builders, all visibly engaged in work that was neither hidden nor polished for presentation. A child growing up in that environment didn't need a deliberate curriculum of "examples of people like you doing things" because the environment provided a steady stream of them as a side effect of ordinary life.
> Modernity has, quietly and without anyone deciding, stripped most of that out. Specialization means work is done in dedicated spaces children don't enter. Efficiency means the struggle and imperfection are hidden — we see finished products, not the messy process. Children are separated from adult work by both physical architecture (offices, factories, professional spaces) and by age-sorted institutions (schools, daycares, age-graded activities). The examples a modern child encounters are either polished professional performances on screens, which don't feel like "people like me," or sanitized peer interactions in institutional settings, which carry the wrong kind of stakes. The rich, ambient example set that used to come free with ordinary life has been almost entirely evacuated.
> And just as with physical exercise, the answer isn't to dismantle modernity — it's to notice what's been lost and build deliberate structures to replace it. The Workshop, in this framing, is the gym for agency and possibility. It's a place specifically designed to reintroduce what the ambient environment no longer provides: mixed-age peers, visible effort and struggle, real projects with real stakes, adults working alongside children in shared space. It doesn't require rejecting the modern world. It just requires acknowledging that the modern world, for all its gains, has a specific deficit that children pay the price for, and that deliberate compensatory infrastructure is the grown-up response.
> This framing also helps you sidestep one of the accusations your work will inevitably face: that you're nostalgic, anti-modern, romanticizing the past. You're not. You're doing exactly what the gym did for physical fitness — accepting that the old environment is gone, that getting it back isn't desirable, and that we need to think carefully about what the old environment was providing that the new one doesn't, and then build specifically to replace it. That's a conservative claim in the good sense of the word: conserving what mattered while keeping what works.
> There's one more implication worth pulling out, because I think it matters for how you frame the Workshop project eventually. The gym works because it's voluntary, self-directed, and responsive to what people actually need rather than what an authority thinks they should want. A gym that operated like a PE class would fail — it would recreate the coercion that makes institutional exercise miserable for many people. Similarly, a workshop that operated like a school would fail, even if the activities inside it looked superficially similar to what children would choose on their own. The form is as important as the content. What makes the Workshop work isn't the tools or the materials or even the presence of peers. It's that everything happening in it is happening because someone chose it. The compensatory institution has to preserve the essential property — voluntary engagement with real projects — that the ambient environment used to provide as a byproduct.

I think my point is subtler than the cobbler’s apprentice studying the master because we still have that, but what we’ve lost is being surrounded by people doing things outside their specialization. The cobbler fixing the neighbouring retailer’s cart wheel because he has the tools. Again, I’m not saying I should do my own plumbing, but having a base expectation that there’s a whole bunch of tasks that one can reasonably try at (and don’t get me started on occupational licensing and what that’s done to generalists and skill exploration)

> The loss isn't the visible expert. We still have those, in fact we have more of them and more specialized ones. The loss is the visible generalist — the person who, when confronted with a problem not in their lane, reaches for whatever tools and knowledge they have and attempts it. The cobbler with the cart wheel isn't modeling cobbling. He's modeling the default posture that when something needs doing, you try, and you only call someone else when you've established you can't.
> A child who grows up watching the adults around them do this constantly — fix things, attempt things, improvise, fail sometimes, succeed often enough — internalizes a particular reflex: when I encounter an unfamiliar problem, the first move is to try. A child who grows up watching adults defer everything outside their specialization to the appropriate credentialed expert internalizes the opposite reflex: when I encounter an unfamiliar problem, the first move is to find the person whose job it is. And this isn't about capability. Both children might be equally smart and equally capable. What's different is the default action, the thing they do without thinking.
> And this is exactly the learned-helplessness / initiative distinction from earlier. It's not that people can't do things. It's that people have been trained out of the reflex to try. The shift from generalist to specialist isn't just economic, it's dispositional — and the dispositional shift is the one that matters for agency.
> Occupational licensing is the formal mechanism that locks the cultural shift into law. Once you need a license to cut hair or braid hair or arrange flowers, the cultural message is no longer "try things" but "don't try things unless you've been authorized." The law follows the culture and then reinforces it, and each generation is raised in a slightly more authorized world than the last. The space for the spontaneous generalist contracts with each cycle. And a child watching this contraction doesn't see people like them attempting things outside their lane, because it's increasingly illegal for people like them to attempt things outside their lane.
> So the Workshop, in this refined version, isn't just about examples of peers doing chosen projects. It's about preserving a space where the generalist reflex can actually be practiced — where a kid can try to fix something they don't know how to fix, build something they don't know how to build, and attempt things that in the outside world would require credentials or permission or liability insurance. That's not a small thing. It might be one of the last places where a child can practice being the kind of person who tries.

As the parent you’re working for the adult version of the child, not yourself and not the current child.



What Fiduciary Means in this Context
------------------------------------

We hold that the parent's role is as fiduciary for the child. The parent's job is not to "do what's best for" the child, by the parent's standards. Nor is it to serve the child's present preferences, but to act as a representative for the future child. To make a best-efforts assessment of what the child's future values and drives will be and to act towards the child now in the way that the child's future self would approve as being directed towards their  flourishing by the future child's own standards.

### No Self-Dealing ###

On the face of it is so obvious as to not seem worthy of mentioning, but as with everything here, the devil’s in the details and there are subtle tensions that are harder to resolve than the cliche of the failed actor parent who pushes their child into acting to live vicariously through them while claiming it’s for the child. But there are genuinely hard cases, like allocating the parent’s time and energy between direct engagement with the child and working (which provides future opportunities for the child). Maybe that’s not strictly a question of self-dealing (although some parents use the claim of the child’s future wellbeing as an excuse to indulge their workaholism).

Distinguish between:

#### Clear Self-Dealing ####

E.g., stage-parent

#### Legitimate Self-Maintenance ####

The parent who works, rests, or sets boundaries to sustain their own capacity to serve the child

#### Genuine Tension (The Unsolvable Middle) ####

There are decisions where the parent's interest and the child's interests are real, legitimate, and in genuine tension and no amount of honesty can fully disentangle them.

Example: A parent who works more to provide financial stability for the child is serving the child's future interests. But a parent who works more because they find work fulfilling and home life draining might be rationalizing self-interest as fiduciary duty. And here's the uncomfortable part — the same parent might be doing both simultaneously, and they might not be able to tell you where one ends and the other begins.
I think the honest answer your framework has to give is that the fiduciary model doesn't resolve these cases — it just requires the parent to notice them and reckon with them rather than hiding behind "it's all for the kids." The duty isn't to be perfect. It's to be honest with yourself about whose interests are actually being served in any given decision. And that's a higher standard than most parenting culture asks for, without being an impossible one.


### What Constrains the Fiduciary (Not Perfection but Honesty) ###

Of course, the child's future self doesn't exist yet and their preferences, values and priorities are not fully formed. So, how does the parent represent that child's future self?

As a child gets older it becomes clearer and clearer what their own values and beliefs are independent of the parent’s. I also think the impact of supporting their values when they diverge becomes greater in rough correlation. Put another way, when a child is an infant they have barely any distinct values so just doing what’s “best for them” is probably pretty close to what their future self would have chosen for themselves, and even if different is unlikely to create long-term problems. And when a child is 18 you can have a pretty good idea of the sort of person they are and the impact of ignoring that is more severe.


Preserving Optionality
----------------------

This is really a corollary of the first point, but is important enough to make explicit.

All about not foreclosing future opportunities

It’s worth a brief aside to say that respecting their values doesn’t mean abdicating responsibility. For example, suppose a 15 year old wants a tattoo but as an older person you have the life experience to know that this is easy to regret. Rather than just overriding their preference, which probably comes from a real place, it’s work with the child to find a way that they can truly express their individuality but in a way that doesn’t foreclose future choices, so maybe letting them get an ugly piercing or dying their hair in some obnoxious colour is a fair substitute.

> Even when you can't yet know what the future self will want, you can distinguish between actions that keep doors open and actions that close them. The fiduciary's job when the child's values are still forming isn't to guess correctly — it's to avoid foreclosing possibilities unnecessarily. This is why reading matters in your framework even though learning isn't the goal. You're not teaching reading because you know the child will value it. You're ensuring the child can choose a life that includes reading, rather than having that choice made for them by neglect.

Navigating the Majority World
=============================

> Your whole argument is about children, but children grow up. And the world they grow up into is the one we've been describing — the credentialed, licensed, institutionally-supervised world where the generalist reflex is suppressed by default. So what happens when a child raised in your framework, with full agency and intact initiative and the habit of trying things, meets the real world at eighteen?
> There are a few versions of the problem. The optimistic version is that these kids will be unusually capable adults who navigate the world better than their peers because they haven't had their agency ground down. Which is probably true in many cases. But there's a harder version: they may find the adult world actively hostile to the dispositions they've developed. The reflex to try things gets punished in many professional environments. The habit of not waiting for permission gets read as insubordination. The instinct to fix problems outside your formal role gets you written up. A person raised to be agentic may find that the structures they have to operate within as adults are specifically designed to suppress exactly the qualities you've cultivated.
> And there's a third, even harder version, which is about belonging. Your framework produces a kind of person who is somewhat rare in the current cultural moment. That person may be psychologically sturdier and more alive, but they may also struggle to find peers, partners, or communities where their dispositions are recognized and welcomed. The Gatto-raised adult is often a lonely adult, not because there's anything wrong with them, but because the world they live in doesn't have many people like them.
> I don't think this defeats your framework. But I think it's a question your framework has to answer honestly, because thoughtful parents are going to ask it. "If I raise my child this way, am I setting them up to be a stranger in their own society?" And I don't think "the world will eventually change" is a sufficient answer, because the parent making the decision today has to act within the world that actually exists.

> somewhere in the project you probably need a short section acknowledging all of this directly. Not defensively — not "here's why my framework won't produce maladjusted adults" — but affirmatively: "here is how the framework prepares children for a world that wasn't built for them, and here is why that preparation is itself part of the gift." That's the answer to the thoughtful parent's question about whether this will leave their child stranded. And it's also, incidentally, an answer to one of the persistent weaknesses of the Holt/Gatto tradition, which tends to be better at critiquing the existing world than at preparing children to live in it.

The Trade-Off is Real and Should Be Made Explicit
-------------------------------------------------

Ok, here’s my initial thought: every choice to do something is also choosing not to do anything else, each choice to become something is a choice not to become other things. Choices made that lead to a person being unusually capable in certain domains necessarily make the person less capable, perhaps *incapable* in other domains. The entrepreneur makes a poor cog in a machine. That’s a real choice with real implications. I hope that it’s a choice that can be made explicit so people choose the orientation that suits them. I make no apology that this framework takes the view that being whole person, with agency and independence, is on net better for most of the likely audience of this work, it’s opinionated but I hope the opinions are overt (and for the most part correct).

> This point — that every choice forecloses others, that making someone unusually capable in one direction necessarily makes them less capable in another — is a clarifying move that your framework probably needs to make explicit. It's honest. It says: this isn't a free lunch. A child raised under this framework will be more of some things and less of others, and the parent choosing this path is making a bet that the trade-off is worth it. And crucially, you're not claiming this is the right choice for everyone. You're claiming it's the right choice for the audience you're actually speaking to, which is parents who have already sensed that the default trade-off the culture offers is producing the wrong result. That's a defensible position and a humble one, and I think it strengthens rather than weakens the argument.
> It also connects back to the fiduciary framing in an interesting way. The parent acting as fiduciary for the child's future self isn't promising to produce a child who will fit anywhere. That's not a guarantee, it's a good-faith act of stewardship under uncertainty. And being honest about the trade-offs is itself part of the fiduciary discipline.

Informed Agency (Knowing What You Are and What the World Is)
------------------------------------------------------------

> A child who grows up with full agency and dignity should also be growing up with a clear-eyed understanding of the world they'll inhabit. That's not a betrayal of the framework. It's the framework in its mature form.
> And there's something genuinely powerful about this. A child raised under your framework doesn't just have agency, they have informed agency. The framework isn't creating people who can't function in the majority world. It's creating people who can function in it without being defined by it.
> This also softens the loneliness problem in a way that matters. Yes, your daughter at twenty-five may find that fewer people share her dispositions than she'd like. She won't be wandering in a hostile world wondering what's wrong with her. She'll know what she is, she'll know what the world is, and she'll know that the gap between them is not her fault and not her problem to fully solve. That's a different kind of strength than "fitting in everywhere," and it's probably the more valuable kind.
